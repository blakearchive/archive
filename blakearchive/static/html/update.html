31 May 2026

The William Blake Archive is pleased to announce the publication of digital editions of Blake's Songs of Innocence Copy E from the Henry W. and Albert A. Berg Collection, New York Public Library, Copy F, from the Houghton Library, Harvard University, and Copy K, from the Carl H. Pforzheimer Collection, New York Public Library.

Blake first published Innocence with thirty-one plates in 1789, the date on its title page. He appears to have printed twenty-three copies that year; three—possibly the first copies of Innocence printed—were in black ink on one side of the leaf, which remained uncolored (Copy U in the Archive is one of the three). Twenty were printed in green, yellow ochre, or raw sienna inks on both sides of octavo size leaves and finished in light watercolor washes. At least four of these were combined with copies of Songs of Experience, printed in 1794, to form Songs of Innocence and of Experience (Copies B-E). For these newly combined copies, Blake moved Plates 34–36 ("The Little Girl Lost" and "The Little Girl Found") to Experience, leaving Innocence with twenty-eight plates. He moved Plates 53 ("The School Boy") and 54 ("The Voice of the Ancient Bard") to Experience by 1818.

Innocence Copy E, printed in 1789 in raw sienna ink on both sides of the leaves, except for the first three plates (Objects 1-3), has thirty-one plates finished in light washes. Innocence Copy F, also printed in 1789 in the same style in raw sienna (Objects 1-3) and yellow ochre (Objects 4-27), has twenty-eight plates and is finished in more opaque colors. It is missing "Holy Thursday," "Nurse’s Song," and the two plates of "The Ecchoing Green," which were extracted by a dealer and inserted into Songs of Innocence and of Experience Copy T. Innocence Copy K was also printed in 1789 in raw sienna on both sides of the leaves, except for the first three plates (Objects 1-3) and the last plate (Object 28). Like Copies E and F, Copy K was initially printed with a full complement of thirty-one plates; the leaves carrying Plates 27/34 and 35/36 were extracted and Plate 27 ("On Anothers Sorrow") was separately printed in a matching ink and added as the last plate. In Copies E, F, and K, the copper plate borders were wiped of ink and so did not print, resulting in impressions that reveal no signs of being printed from metal plates, appearing instead like manuscript pages.

Most of the impressions comprising Copy K were printed in the same session as Copy E, but the coloring style is more sophisticated in that many texts are in part strengthened or over-written in black, red, raw sienna, or green with a pen or small brush. The fine black lines are probably ink, but other colors may be water color wash. See the Editors' Notes on Objects 2-13, 15-26, and 28. This strengthening of the texts, as well as some of the tinting in water colors, particularly the washes in text areas, were very probably added a least a few years after the 1789 printing of the plates, probably c. 1795, and are in the same style as Innocence Copy L. The overwriting seems more decorative than attempts at salvaging illegible or poorly printed texts, as was often the primary reason Blake strengthened texts in this manner (see for example Songs of Innocence and of Experience Copy E).

With these copies—which have not been reproduced before—the Archive continues to realize its objective of publishing exemplary copies of each illuminated book from each of its printings and expanding the Blake canon by presenting works previously not available for study by the general public. The Archive now contains fully searchable and scalable digital editions of over 135 copies of Blake's nineteen illuminated books. Users may view color corrected digital images of Blake's works at their true size, and enlarge and rotate these images to examine texts and illustrations in detail. The images are accompanied by diplomatic transcriptions and editors' notes, as well as illustration descriptions that make it possible to search Blake's works for visual motifs. The Archive also provides full bibliographic and provenance information for each digital edition it publishes.

As always, the William Blake Archive is a free site, imposing no access restrictions and charging no subscription fees.
